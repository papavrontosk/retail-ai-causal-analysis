Panel balance: The panel is unbalanced. Stores have between 20 and 32 reported months, reflecting differences in opening dates, closing dates, and intermittent missing reports that were removed. No two stores share the exact same set of calendar months, so a balanced panel identity cannot hold.

Handling the Unbalanced Panel

The unbalanced structure arises from three sources: (i) staggered store opening and closing dates, (ii) months with report_flag = 0 excluded by construction, and (iii) the pre/post balance requirement that removes treated stores entering too late or too early in the dataset. In all subsequent regressions, fixed-effects and first-difference estimators naturally accommodate unbalanced panels. Month fixed effects absorb aggregate time trends regardless of which stores contribute observations to each calendar month.

Q2 Time Trends by Treatment Status Group

Each store-month observation is assigned one of three mutually exclusive status labels:

  • Never treated – stores that never adopted the personalization engine (treated = 0).
  • Not yet live – treated stores in calendar months before their go_live_month.
  • Live – treated stores in months ≥ go_live_month.

Why Pooled Time Patterns Alone Do Not Establish Causality

The charts show a visually compelling pattern: once stores go live on the engine, their mean ln(sales) trend accelerates compared to the never-treated group. However, this observation is insufficient for a causal claim for the following reasons:

  1. Selection bias: The rollout was not randomised. Stores selected into (or selected for) treatment may have better managers, higher digital readiness, or be located in faster-growing markets—all factors correlated with both ai_share and sales growth.
  2. Pre-existing trends: The "Not yet live" group already has somewhat higher ln(sales) than "Never treated" before go-live, suggesting that treated stores were growing faster even before treatment.
  3. Confounding time shocks: Calendar-time events (seasonality, competitor actions, advertising campaigns) affect all groups simultaneously. Without a control group untouched by these shocks, the group trajectories conflate treatment effects with common time trends.
  4. Compositional changes: As months pass, the "Not yet live" group shrinks and the "Live" group grows. The rising mean in the "Live" group partly reflects a changing composition of stores, not just within-store changes.

Establishing causality requires isolating within-store, within-time variation using techniques such as fixed effects, first differences, or credible instrumental variables—all of which are implemented in Questions 3–8.

What Do We Gain and Lose by Reducing T?

Gains: (1) A perfectly balanced panel eliminates any attrition bias or selection driven by which stores survive long enough to report. (2) Comparisons across estimators are cleaner because every store contributes the same number of months. (3) Avoids the compositional change problem whereby month fixed effects in the unbalanced panel are identified off different store samples at different dates.

Losses: (1) Drastic loss of sample size—from 77 stores to 12. This severely reduces statistical power and makes estimates unstable, as seen in the FD-B results. (2) External validity is limited to the narrow window and the atypical subset of stores that remained fully observed. (3) The 12-month window may straddle the go-live period differently across stores, confounding the dynamic profile. In sum, the reduced-T approach buys panel purity at a steep cost in precision and generalisability.

Which Strategy Is More Convincing: Reduce T or Reduce N?

Preferred strategy: Reduce N (miss ≤ 4). Reducing N retains a longer time horizon (20–32 months), which is essential for identifying dynamic effects in FD and preserving enough between-period variation for time fixed effects. The quality restriction (missing ≤ 4) removes stores whose missingness may be endogenous (e.g., stores that stop reporting during difficult months), thus reducing measurement bias without sacrificing the temporal depth needed for causal identification. The FD-B reduced-N estimate (2.52%) is close to the FE reduced-N (2.69%) and broadly consistent with the main sample estimates, suggesting robustness. By contrast, reducing T to 12 months collapses the sample to only 12 stores, causing extreme imprecision and fragility, as evidenced by the sign-changing and insignificant FD-B reduced-T coefficients.

The long difference estimate (3.56%) is larger than the FE and FD estimates from the same reduced-T sample, and considerably larger than the main-sample FD-B. As the lecture notes, the long difference is a useful first pass because it sweeps away measurement noise in intermediate months and focuses on the total accumulated change. However, it is typically a weaker causal design for three reasons:

  1. No control for intermediate confounders: Any event that happened between March 2023 and February 2024 and that is correlated with both ai_share expansion and sales growth gets folded into the estimate. FD and FE models, by using month-by-month variation, can include month fixed effects that absorb time-varying aggregate shocks.
  2. Cross-sectional identification only: With only 12 observations in a cross-section, the long difference relies entirely on between-store variation in the 11-month change in ai_share. This is more vulnerable to selection bias (stores that expanded ai_share faster may be systematically different).
  3. Tiny sample: With N = 12, standard errors are unreliable, inference is fragile, and OLS cannot be clustered. The apparent precision in the t-statistic is misleading.
Q9 Dynamic Analysis: Pre-Trends, Causality, and Specification Sensitivity

1. Evidence on Pre-Trends and Reverse Causality

The one-period lead (ρ₁ = Δai_sharet+1) in both FD-A and FD-B is small and statistically insignificant:

  • FD-A: ρ₁ = 0.000116, p = 0.611
  • FD-B: ρ₁ = 0.000031, p = 0.917

This is the key diagnostic for pre-trends and reverse causality. A large, significant positive lead coefficient would indicate either: (a) sales start rising before ai_share increases (anticipation effects or reverse causality—managers roll out the engine in stores that are already improving), or (b) a spurious pre-existing trend is driving both. The insignificant lead in both specifications is reassuring: there is no evidence that sales move ahead of ai_share changes. This supports the interpretation of the contemporaneous coefficient as reflecting a genuine, at-least-partly-causal response rather than a pre-trend.

2. Immediate or Lagged Dynamics?

The contemporaneous coefficient (β₀) is the largest and most precisely estimated in both specifications. The one-period lag (β₁) is positive in both FD-A and FD-B but only marginally significant in FD-A (p = 0.071). The two-period lag (β₂) is positive but insignificant. This pattern suggests:

  • Most of the effect of ai_share on sales is contemporaneous: personalisation works quickly, likely because it directly affects conversion rates and basket size within the same month.
  • There is a modest but decaying delayed component—consistent with a brief customer habit-formation or re-engagement effect over the following month or two.
  • The 3-month cumulative effect is approximately 50–60% larger than the contemporaneous effect alone (0.00196 vs 0.00115 in FD-A), indicating that fully capturing the effect requires including the lag structure.

3. Does Adding Store-Specific Trends Change the Estimate?

Going from FD-A to FD-B (adding store dummies), the cumulative effect decreases from 1.96% to 1.69%—a reduction of about 14%. This downward revision is modest and statistically compatible with sampling variation, but it is consistent with the hypothesis that treated stores had mildly upward-trending sales independently of the algorithm rollout. FD-B is the more conservative and theoretically defensible specification.

4. Better Design or Over-Control?

Adding store-specific linear trends (FD-B) is an improvement over FD-A if: treated stores had systematically different underlying sales trends that could bias FD-A upward. The 14% reduction in the coefficient suggests this is a real concern, not negligible, but also not severe enough to fully explain away the effect.

However, there is a risk of over-control: if the algorithm genuinely caused store-level trend changes (e.g., by permanently shifting customer behaviour), then absorbing store trends would partial out part of the true causal effect. Given the gradual, ramp-up nature of ai_share adoption (from 0% to 60–90% over 12–18 months), this concern is legitimate. The "true" effect likely lies between FD-A and FD-B—around 1.7–2.0% per 10pp—and we prefer FD-B as the conservative lower bound.

Best estimate. Our preferred specification—a first-difference regression with store-specific linear trends (FD-B) on the full 77-store panel—estimates that a 10 percentage-point increase in ai_share raises monthly net sales by approximately 1.7% within three months. This figure represents a conservative lower bound; the simpler FD-A estimate suggests 2.0% and the FE estimate 2.5%. We recommend citing the FD-B range of 1.7–2.0% in external communications.

Why this specification. FD-B controls for both store-specific permanent differences and store-specific growth trajectories, and it passes a pre-trend test: sales do not move ahead of ai_share changes, which would have signalled reverse causality. The main unbalanced panel (77 stores, 2,053 obs) gives the best statistical power, and sensitivity checks on cleaner sub-samples (reduced-N) are broadly consistent.

Causal caveats. The rollout was not randomised—waves were assigned partly by management discretion. Stores that adopted early or received override decisions may differ systematically from late adopters in ways our controls cannot fully capture. The pre-trend check is reassuring but not conclusive proof of exogeneity. Additionally, ai_share measures transaction volume through the engine, not algorithm quality; some stores may have high ai_share simply because they defaulted all traffic through it without any genuine personalisation uplift.

Sample strategy. We prefer the full unbalanced panel over the reduced-T (12 stores, 12 months) or reduced-N (21 stores) alternatives. The full panel provides the temporal depth and cross-store variation needed for credible identification. Reduced-T buys balance at the cost of near-zero statistical power. Reduced-N improves data quality marginally but excludes two-thirds of treated stores without strong justification. The full panel, with its strict FD and pre-trend diagnostics, remains the most informative design available with current data.
